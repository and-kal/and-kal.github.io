This review may contain spoilers.

»Young Soul Rebels« tells the story of two pirate radio DJs, Caz and Chris, aka Soul Patrol, and their attempt to solve the murder of their friend TJ, who was killed while cruising for gay sex in a park at night. Director Isaac Julien is certainly best known in the context of video art, through his video installations and photographs, as well as through his essayistic documentaries (e.g. about Frantz Fanon or Derek Jarman). His first classic feature film therefore seems somewhat out of the ordinary - even though Julien impressively demonstrates his artistic strengths from these other media in this film, too.
While the plot, set in the late 1970s and around the Queen's Silver Jubilee, is reasonably exciting and engaging, it must be said that the feature film or crime thriller aspect of this movie is perhaps not its real core, and that other aspects of it are what really make »Young Soul Rebels« worth watching. Much more interesting than the plot are the film's many other threads, in which Julien touches on various political developments in the UK in the 1970s and socio-political issues and conflicts.
As in many of Julien's films, the subject matter naturally revolves around queer (gay) and Black identities and, building on that, society, subjectification and discrimination. However, the way in which these themes are dealt with is particularly stirring as it does not indulge in superficial warnings and overly obvious references to injustices, but also finds surprising images for the wrongs of a nationalist, capitalist society, both then and now. In one scene, Caz's fling Tracy shows him the unglamorous office of celebrated black radio presenter Jeff Kane - a role model for the two pirate DJs - who resides in a tiny cubicle and, despite his reputation, only ever manages to scrape by on three-month contracts. There is also a scene in which Tracy criticises Chris' punk boyfriend Billibud for wearing £20 Vivien Westwood shirts just to look subcultural and give himself an air of anarchism. Julien shows how discrimination against minorities surely does come from the side of white skinheads, National Front sympathisers or patriots from the majority society, but is also so deeply rooted in society that it occurs within communities affected by racism and classism, and create inner conflicts and tensions, which Julien depicts in their inclemency: for example, in the mistrust of Caz and Chris's Jamaican-born work colleagues towards the two of them because of their ›half-caste‹ status and them being gay.
In addition to this kind of political statement in film form, Julien's cinematographic signature in the film is also remarkable. The sensuality of the camera work in the film is sometimes reminiscent of Claire Denis' films - for example, in the interrogation scene in which Caz is accused of murder and you can downright see his terror and anger written all over his skin. Or in the real-time sex scene of Chris and Billibud, which lasts about a minute and a half and presents an impressively credible picture of male sexuality and orgasm.
Even if the plot and dramaturgy of the film are not exactly flawless, there are still enough aspects that make it worthwhile, allowing Julien's strengths as a visual artist to shine through and translate powerfully into a more classic narrative cinema. What's more, the film recalls traditional subcultural practices such as pirate radio and underground parties and provides a retrospective view of the late 1970s in England from an unusual (i.e. not punk-centric) perspective, which at the same time points to the time of its creation (the 1990s) and beyond to the present day, where society still finds itself on similar socio-political battlegrounds.
